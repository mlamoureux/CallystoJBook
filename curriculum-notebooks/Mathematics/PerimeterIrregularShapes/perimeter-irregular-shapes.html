Irregular Shapes¶

Irregular Shape Introduction¶

To better understand an irregular shape, we will first define what a regular shape is.

  • A Regular Shape is a shape that has sides and angles that are equal.

  • An Irregular Shape is a shape that has sides and angles of any size.

This idea is easier to see through visuals. So, the following shape would be a regular shape:

There are actually two ways to find the length of \(z\):

Here, we start by using \(z\) as the “\(A\)” value, \(4\)cm as the “\(B\)” value, and \(5\)cm as the “\(C\)” value. Now, we can calculate for \(z\) by doing:

The Pythagorean Method¶

$$¶

\begin{align*} z &= \sqrt {(5\textrm{cm})^2 - (4\textrm{cm})^2} \ &= \sqrt {25\textrm{cm}^2 - 16\textrm{cm}^2}\ &= \sqrt {9\textrm{cm}^2} \end{align*} $$ So, we know that the length of \(z = 3\)cm

Here, we start by looking at the length on the far right side of the shape. We see that it is \(10\)cm long. This tells us that the length of the dotted line on the left must equal \(10\)cm. Then, we can do:

The Known Values Method¶

$$¶

\begin{align*} z &= 10 - 7 \end{align*} $$ So, we know that the length of \(z = 3\)cm

Finally, it’s up to you to calculate the area of the whole shape! To do this, you have to:

Add the Areas of the Shapes Inside
NOTE: We only add the areas of shapes that are inside our original shape. So, for this example, we would not add the pink triangle on the bottom right.

Subtract the Areas of the Shapes That are Holes
NOTE: We only subtract the areas of shapes that are not inside our original shape. So, for this example, we would subtract the pink triangle on the bottom right.
